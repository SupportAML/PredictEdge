# Kalshi Tax Reporting: The Complete 2026 Guide

If you traded on Kalshi this year, you probably already know the bad news: **Kalshi does not issue 1099-B forms** for event contract trading activity.

Unlike traditional brokerages that send you a neat tax statement every January, Kalshi only issues:

- **1099-MISC** — for referral bonuses
- **1099-INT** — for interest earned on uninvested cash balances

Your actual trading gains and losses? You're on your own.

The IRS has issued **zero formal guidance** on prediction market tax treatment as of 2026. That doesn't mean you don't owe taxes — it means the reporting burden falls entirely on you.

## How Prediction Market Trades Are Taxed

Prediction market contracts on Kalshi are classified as event contracts regulated by the CFTC. While the IRS hasn't published specific rules, the consensus among tax professionals is:

- **Gains from winning contracts** are taxable income
- **Losses from losing contracts** may be deductible (subject to limitations)
- **Short-term vs. long-term** distinction applies — contracts held less than one year are taxed at ordinary income rates
- **Each contract settlement is a taxable event** — you can't just look at your net balance

## The Manual Process (What Most Traders Do Now)

1. Export your trade history as CSV from Kalshi's account settings
2. Map each trade to its settlement outcome
3. Calculate cost basis per contract (price paid x quantity)
4. Calculate proceeds per contract (settlement value x quantity)
5. Determine gain/loss for each position
6. Aggregate into Schedule D format
7. Hand it to your CPA and pray

This process takes hours for active traders. CPA firms like Camuso CPA and Monaco CPA charge **$500+** just for Kalshi tax prep because the data is so messy.

## FAQ

**Q: Do I really need to report Kalshi gains?**
A: Yes. All income is taxable. The IRS can match Kalshi's reporting (1099-MISC/INT) to your return, and unreported trading income is a red flag.

**Q: Can I deduct my Kalshi losses?**
A: Likely yes, as capital losses — but consult a tax professional for your specific situation.
